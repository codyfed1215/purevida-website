Texas heat doesn't take breaks, and neither do the crews running warehouses, manufacturing plants, distribution centers, machine shops, and auto operations. The work is physical. The shifts are long. The hydration demand is real.

Most operations didn't choose 5-gallon jug delivery — they inherited it. It's been there since before the current GM started, and it'll be there until somebody bothers to change it. The problem is, *somebody bothers to change it* usually means somebody just hurt their back lifting a 42-pound jug onto a top-load cooler.

The other situation PureVida sees just as often: there's already a water setup on the wall — a bottom-tier point-of-use filter, a wall-mounted fountain, a hand-me-down cooler — and nobody on the floor drinks from it. The crew doesn't trust the taste. They don't trust the filter changes. They don't trust the unit itself. So the team buys cases of bottled water out of pocket, refills jugs at home, or — worst case — just doesn't drink enough water through a 95-degree afternoon.

## What's Actually Wrong With Most Plant-Floor Water Setups

Two patterns show up across Texas warehouses, plants, and shops:

- **Inherited jug delivery.** 42-pound lifts onto top-load coolers. Open-air reservoirs. Late or short deliveries. Workers' comp exposure stacked on top of every other incident category.
- **A setup the team won't use.** A cheap wall filter or a forgotten fountain that nobody trusts. The water is technically there. The crew is still dehydrated.

Either way, the result on the floor is the same: people aren't drinking enough water, and the operation is paying for a system that isn't doing its job.

## The Hidden Cost of Jug Delivery in Physical Workplaces

Across a busy warehouse or shop floor, the jug lifts add up fast — and so do the costs:

- **Real workers' comp exposure** — overexertion injuries (which include lifting injuries) are consistently among the largest categories of nonfatal workplace injuries. A 42-pound jug lifted overhead onto a top-load cooler is exactly that kind of incident waiting to happen.
- **Real lost productivity** — every minute someone is wrestling with a jug is a minute not on the floor.
- **Real downtime** — when the delivery is late or the order is short, the crew runs out.
- **Real liability** — hot-water burn risk from tipped jug coolers in high-traffic environments.
- **Plant managers playing plumber** — when something fails, the burden lands on whoever's nearest. That's not their job.

And the water itself? It sits in an open-air reservoir for days. In a warehouse environment with airborne dust, that's not the water any crew should be drinking on a 95-degree Texas afternoon.
